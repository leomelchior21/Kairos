# Inquiry Example Flows

These examples document how Kai should behave as an inquiry engine. The final answer appears only after evidence has been collected and the student completes a synthesis challenge.

## 1. What is solarpunk?

Question type: concept

Key concepts:
- Sustainable future
- Technology with nature
- Community design
- Hopeful social imagination

Common misconceptions:
- Solarpunk is only solar panels.
- Solarpunk means rejecting technology.
- Solarpunk is just fantasy with no real-world action.

Sequence of steps:
1. Hook, multiple choice: "Which lens fits this idea best?" Options compare style, future, gadget, or weather.
2. Prior knowledge, true/false: "Solarpunk combines nature and technology."
3. Guided discovery, match: Match "green roofs", "solar panels", and "community gardens" to the ideas they support.
4. Checkpoint, multiple choice: Pick the strongest reason solarpunk is more than decoration.
5. Synthesis challenge, fill in the blanks: "Solarpunk imagines a ___ future where ___ and ___ work together."
6. Final reveal: Confirm the definition.

Mastery updates:
- Correct hook: lost -> emerging
- Correct true/false: emerging -> developing
- Correct match/checkpoint: developing -> secure
- Completed synthesis: secure -> ready_to_synthesize

Final fill-in-the-blank challenge:
`Solarpunk imagines a ___ future where ___ and ___ work together.`

Final revealed answer:
Solarpunk is a cultural and design movement that imagines hopeful, sustainable futures where technology, nature, and community work together.

## 2. What is the tallest mountain in the world?

Question type: fact

Key concepts:
- Measurement
- Height above sea level
- Evidence criteria
- Mount Everest

Common misconceptions:
- "Tallest" always means the same measurement.
- A fact does not need a criterion.
- Base-to-peak and above-sea-level measurements always give the same result.

Sequence of steps:
1. Hook, multiple choice: "What must we choose first?" Options include height rule, color, country size, or age.
2. Prior knowledge, true/false: "A measurement rule changes the answer."
3. Guided discovery, multiple choice: Choose "height above sea level" as the deciding evidence.
4. Checkpoint, short answer: "Name the measurement clue in five words or less."
5. Synthesis challenge, fill in the blanks: "Using height above ___, the tallest mountain is ___."
6. Final reveal: Confirm the fact and mention the measurement caveat.

Mastery updates:
- Correct hook: lost -> emerging
- Correct true/false: emerging -> developing
- Correct evidence choice: developing -> secure
- Completed synthesis: secure -> ready_to_synthesize

Final fill-in-the-blank challenge:
`Using height above ___, the tallest mountain is ___.`

Final revealed answer:
The tallest mountain above sea level is Mount Everest. If measured from base to peak, Mauna Kea is often discussed separately.

## 3. What was the Cold War?

Question type: concept

Key concepts:
- Ideological conflict
- United States
- Soviet Union
- Nuclear tension
- Proxy wars
- Alliances

Common misconceptions:
- It was a single normal battle.
- "Cold" means weather.
- Only soldiers were involved.

Sequence of steps:
1. Hook, multiple choice: "Which clue matters most?" Options include rivalry, weather, one battle, or sports.
2. Prior knowledge, true/false: "The Cold War had tension without direct superpower war."
3. Guided discovery, match: Match "USA", "Soviet Union", and "proxy wars" to their roles.
4. Checkpoint, multiple choice: Pick the clearest description of the conflict.
5. Synthesis challenge, fill in the blanks: "The Cold War was a ___ conflict between the ___ and the ___."
6. Final reveal: Confirm the historical explanation.

Mastery updates:
- Correct hook: lost -> emerging
- Correct true/false: emerging -> developing
- Correct match/checkpoint: developing -> secure
- Completed synthesis: secure -> ready_to_synthesize

Final fill-in-the-blank challenge:
`The Cold War was a ___ conflict between the ___ and the ___.`

Final revealed answer:
The Cold War was a long political, ideological, and military rivalry after World War II, mainly between the United States and the Soviet Union, fought through pressure, alliances, arms races, and proxy conflicts rather than direct war between them.
